Title: Epistemological Disruptions and Ethical Asymmetries: Generative AI as a Historical Method

Section 1: The Traditional Historical Method – Hermeneutics and Source Criticism
Historical methodology, since the 19th-century professionalization of the discipline by Leopold von Ranke and his contemporaries, has rested upon two foundational pillars: source criticism (Quellenkritik) and hermeneutic interpretation. Source criticism involves the systematic evaluation of primary documents for authenticity, provenance, and authorial bias. A historian cross-references fiscal records, personal correspondence, and material artifacts to construct a probabilistic account of past events. Hermeneutics, following Dilthey and Gadamer, adds the recursive movement between part and whole—understanding a decree requires understanding the monarch’s worldview, which in turn is inferred from many such decrees. This circular process is time-intensive, iterative, and fundamentally human: empathy with historical actors, contextual intuition, and the acknowledgment of interpretive limits are core to the craft.

Section 2: How Generative AI Models Process Historical Corpora
Generative artificial intelligence, specifically transformer-based large language models (LLMs) like GPT-4 and its successors, operates on a radically different principle. These models ingest digitized historical corpora (e.g., Early English Books Online, Gallica, or the HathiTrust Digital Library) and learn statistical distributions of tokens—words, punctuation, and formatting markers. Through multi-head self-attention mechanisms, the model predicts plausible sequences given a prompt. There is no semantic understanding of “19th-century liberalism” as a historically contingent ideology; instead, the model captures co-occurrence patterns and latent vector relationships. When asked to “write a summary of parliamentary debates on the Factory Acts of 1833,” the LLM does not retrieve a memory but generates a high-probability string based on training data. This process can produce syntactically fluent, citation-like text that mimics historical discourse without any referential grounding in actual archives.

Section 3: Epistemological Ruptures – Plausibility Versus Verifiability
The introduction of generative AI into historical research creates an epistemological rupture traditionally framed around truth correspondence. In Rankean historiography, the goal is wie es eigentlich gewesen (“as it actually happened”)—an asymptotically approachable ideal anchored by archival evidence. Generative models, however, optimize for plausibility given a training distribution, not verification against unseen primary sources. Consequently, an LLM might generate a convincing diary entry from a Victorian factory worker that aligns statistically with 1840s working-class vocabularies but describes an event that never occurred. The algorithm cannot distinguish between a genuine source and a statistically typical fabrication. This challenges two core epistemic virtues of history: evidential accountability (each claim traceable to a source) and falsifiability (the claim can be disproven by counter-evidence). When historians use LLMs for synthetic data or writing assistance, they risk importing “hallucinated” facts that are structurally indistinguishable from accurate summaries.

Section 4: Ethical Asymmetries – Authorship, Anachronism, and Labor
Beyond epistemology, generative AI raises distinct ethical asymmetries not present in prior computational methods (e.g., keyword search or topic modeling). First, authorship attribution becomes ambiguous. If a historian prompts an LLM to “rewrite this paragraph in the style of E.P. Thompson,” and then edits the output, where does original scholarly contribution reside? Historical journals now require AI disclosure statements, but the boundary between AI-assisted synthesis and AI-generated content remains vague. Second, anachronistic reasoning may be amplified: LLMs trained on contemporary internet text inadvertently inject present-day moral frameworks (e.g., modern concepts of gender identity or postcolonial critique) into outputs about pre-modern societies, flattening historical alterity. Third, labor ethics: the training data for commercial LLMs often includes copyrighted historical monographs and digitized archives without proper licensing or compensation to publishers and authors. Using such models for historical research thus embeds a structural extraction of value from previous scholarly labor, raising questions of academic solidarity and fair use.

Section 5: Pragmatic Recommendations for Algorithmic Chunking in AI-Augmented Historiography
Given these challenges, any semantic chunking algorithm designed to process historical texts generated or assisted by AI must incorporate context-sensitive boundaries. Chunk boundaries should be triggered not only by lexical shifts (e.g., from “methodology” to “ethics”) but also by epistemic modality markers—phrases such as “the model predicts,” “in the training data,” or “no archival evidence supports.” Furthermore, historical claims that include direct quotations or citations to physical repositories (e.g., “Kew, FO 83/2211”) should be segmented into separate chunks from synthetic narrative summaries. Recommended chunk size: 500–1500 tokens, with overlap windows of 20% to preserve cross-chunk references to key historiographic debates (e.g., the Rankean versus postmodern positions). Algorithms should also flag chunks containing high statistical likelihood but low verifiable grounding—a novel “plausibility score” distinct from semantic similarity.
Title: Fronto-Temporal Dysconnectivity and Predictive Coding Failure: Two Models of Auditory Verbal Hallucinations

Section 1: Clinical Phenomenology of Auditory Verbal Hallucinations
Auditory verbal hallucinations (AVHs) in schizophrenia spectrum disorders are defined as perception-like experiences occurring without an external stimulus, characterized by clear sensory quality (voices speaking, whispering, or shouting) and volitional autonomy (the person does not control the onset or content). Prevalence rates exceed 60% among individuals with chronic schizophrenia. Phenomenologically, AVHs vary along three axes: (1) complexity—from single words to full conversational exchanges; (2) valence—from benign commentary to derogatory commands; and (3) spatial localization—internal (inside the head) versus external (across the room). Unlike daydreaming or intrusive thoughts, AVHs possess compelling perceptual realism, often leading patients to search for hidden speakers or recording devices. Clinical distinction from non-pathological voice-hearing (e.g., in grief or highly creative individuals) hinges on distress, duration, and loss of reality testing.

Section 2: Structural and Functional Neuroimaging Findings – The Fronto-Temporal Hypothesis
The fronto-temporal hypothesis has dominated neurobiological accounts of AVHs since the 1990s. Structural MRI studies consistently report reduced gray matter volume in the left superior temporal gyrus (STG)—which houses primary auditory cortex—and in the inferior frontal gyrus (Broca’s area, involved in speech production) among hallucination-prone patients. Diffusion tensor imaging reveals decreased fractional anisotropy in the arcuate fasciculus, the white matter tract connecting temporal and frontal language regions. Functionally, during acute AVHs, positron emission tomography (PET) and fMRI show paradoxical co-activation: Broca’s area exhibits increased metabolic demand (as if inner speech is being generated), while the STG responds as though external speech is being perceived. However, effective connectivity analysis (dynamic causal modeling) indicates that this co-activation occurs without the normal frontal-to-temporal inhibitory modulation seen in non-hallucinating controls. In essence, the brain listens to its own inner speech but misattributes the source due to disrupted connectivity.

Section 3: Predictive Coding and the Hierarchical Bayesian Brain
An alternative, computationally precise model derives from predictive coding theory. In this framework, the brain continuously generates hierarchical predictions about sensory input, from low-level acoustical features (e.g., pitch and loudness) to high-level semantic content (e.g., “this is a sentence about weather”). Sensory evidence is compared against predictions; mismatches produce prediction errors that update internal models. Crucially, precision weighting—the estimated reliability of each prediction error—determines whether bottom-up sensory signals or top-down priors dominate perception. In AVHs, computational psychiatry models propose pathologically elevated precision of top-down linguistic priors combined with attenuated precision of bottom-up auditory evidence. Thus, even in silence, a strong prior expectation of hearing “a human voice saying something relevant” generates a perceptually vivid hallucination, while the lack of sensory evidence (no actual sound) fails to correct the inference. This explains why closing the eyes (reducing visual priors) does not stop AVHs, whereas engaging in demanding auditory tasks (e.g., listening to real speech through headphones) can temporarily suppress them.

Section 4: Cross-Modality Interactions – Visual, Somatosensory, and Interoceptive Contributions
While AVHs are auditory by definition, recent research highlights cross-modality interactions. Patients with schizophrenia show increased functional connectivity between auditory cortex and the visual word form area (VWFA) during hallucinatory episodes, suggesting that sublexical orthographic processing (imagined text or lip-reading representations) may prime auditory experiences. Similarly, somatosensory hallucinations (e.g., feeling unseen fingers touching the skin) co-occur with AVHs in roughly 30% of cases, and intercorrelated activation of the secondary somatosensory cortex (SII) with the STG has been reported. Interoceptive signals—cardiac and respiratory rhythms—also modulate AVH intensity: higher heart rate variability preceding a hallucinatory episode predicts greater phenomenological detail, likely due to aberrant signaling from the insular cortex to language-generating regions. These cross-modal findings challenge the purely “auditory” label; instead, AVHs may emerge from a generalized failure of modality-specific prediction error attenuation, with auditory-verbal content being the most common expression because of human evolution’s reliance on spoken communication.

Section 5: Implications for Computational Chunking of Clinical Text Corpora
For a semantic chunking algorithm applied to clinical interview transcripts, diagnostic notes, or neuroimaging reports on AVHs, three boundaries should be explicitly modeled. First, separate chunks should isolate first-person phenomenological descriptions (“the voice calls me worthless every evening”) from third-person neurobiological explanations (“increased STG-Broca’s connectivity”). Mixing these within a single chunk obscures the distinct epistemic status (subjective vs. objective) required for clinical reasoning. Second, chunk boundaries should align with statistical versus mechanistic claims: a sentence reporting correlation (e.g., “fractional anisotropy correlates with PANSS hallucination score”) belongs apart from a sentence postulating causal mechanism (e.g., “precision weighting of linguistic priors causes top-down capture”). Third, given the predictive coding model’s mathematical formulation (free energy minimization), chunks containing equations or Bayesian terminology should remain intact without splitting across parameters. Optimal chunking strategy: dynamic sliding windows with sentence-level semantic change detection, using cosine similarity of SBERT embeddings thresholded at 0.65; when similarity falls below this for three consecutive sentences, a new chunk is initiated. Validation should use a gold standard corpus of hallucination research abstracts manually annotated for topic shifts by two independent clinical researchers, with Cohen’s kappa ≥ 0.80.
